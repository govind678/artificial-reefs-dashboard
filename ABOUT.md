# Artificial Reefs of the World — Interactive Map

An open, interactive world map documenting 162 artificial reef sites across 40 ocean regions and 6 continents, as of March 25, 2026. Built by the Octopolis team as a public resource for researchers, divers, conservationists, artists, and anyone drawn to what's happening beneath the surface.

## What It Does

Visualizes the global landscape of artificial reef projects — from sunken warships and 3D-printed coral substrates to underwater sculpture parks and oyster restoration programs. Each reef includes a detailed card showing its name, organization, location, geographic region, category, description, size, design approach, and a link to learn more.

## Features

- **15 reef categories** with color-coded markers and per-category counts
- **Real-time search** across reef name, location, organization, and geographic region — search "Asia", "Caribbean", "Indian Ocean" and results appear instantly
- **Smart filtering** that narrows visible categories as you type, and restores your previous selection when you clear
- **Region-aware data**: every reef tagged with its continent and nearest major water body (e.g., "Asia / Bali Sea", "North America / Caribbean Sea")
- **Responsive** on desktop and mobile
- **Free and open**: hosted on Cloudflare Pages for free, no account required

## Why This Exists

More than 30,000 artificial reef sites exist worldwide, yet no centralized global registry exists. The science is broad and growing, with over 270 peer-reviewed papers spanning ecology, carbon sequestration, fisheries economics, community transformation, and materials science, but the knowledge remains fragmented across journals, government reports, and isolated project pages.

This map is a first step toward making that global picture visible. The answers matter not only to scientists, but also to divers, coastal communities, artists, engineers, and anyone concerned with marine restoration. Artificial reefs sit at the intersection of ecology, economics, art, and community, and the more clearly we understand their successes, failures, and long-term impacts, the better equipped we are to design what comes next.

## What Is Octopolis

This project is part of **Octopolis** — a large-scale participatory art installation centered on an octopus sculpture designed first for public engagement, then for long-term deployment as an artificial reef structure. Its underwater phase is intended to provide hard substrate, habitat complexity, and shelter that may support coral attachment and broader marine colonization over time.

This research compares reef designs, materials, ecological outcomes, and site conditions so Octopolis can be placed in a marine environment suited to its structure and to its ecological and cultural role as public underwater art.
